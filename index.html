TFUHP: Tools For UNDERSTANDING Highway Performance

“In many industrialized nations today, highways present engineeres and governments with formidable challenges relating to saftey, sustainability, environmental impacts, congestion mitigation, and deteriorating infrastructure. As a result, highways are often viewed from a perspective of the many challenges as they present as opposed to the benefits that they provide. (Mannering and Washburn 2017)” This website deals mainly with safety and congestion mitigation issues.

Let’s get started with investigating traffic breakdown or the onset of congestion. As all drivers know from experience, the highway will be is a congested depending on how many vehicles are on the highway. They may observe that a highway with seemingly the same number of the vehicles on the highway may or may not be in a congested state. Some drivers may alter their trip plan to avoid dealing with a congestion event or the possibility of being involved in a crash. What are these drivers doing? They are: (1) collecting data, (2) analyzing the data, and (3) making a decision based on their analyses. For example, a person needs grocercies. There is a strong possibility that the person may decide (3) not to travel during rush hour because (1) the traffic will be too heavy and (2) the chance being delayed or being involved in an accident is deemed unacceptable at this time of day. The extra time of travel and/or risk of being involved in a crash are predicted costs and measures of highway performance.

To develop a successful transportaion operation and management plan, there is a clear need for understanding of the process being operated and managed. The decision-making framework for individual travelers described in the preceding paragraph will be used. In place of a subjective analysis, analytical tools will be used to assign values to the probability of a traffic breakdown event, \(\pi = P(U < u^*)\) where \(u^*\) is a critical speed that separates a congested state from a free-flow (uncongested) state, and expected time of delay, \(E(D)\), measured in units of time.

There are uncertainties associated with these highway performance predictions. Making a reliable prediction is not easy. There are number of issues associated with data collection and model selection that must be addressed. Take model selection. The chance of traffic breakdown \(\pi\) can be estimated as \(\pi = P(Q = q)\) or \(\pi = P(K = k)\). Capital letters, \(D, K, U\) and \(Q\) are assumed to be random numbers and lower case letters, \(d, k, u\) and \(q\) are constants. Which is better model or does it make any difference?

To answer this question and questions like it, the root cause of traffic breakdown must be identified and understood. To aid in this understanding, various analytical tools are used and the simplest model structures are sought. For example, by counting \(n\) vehicles over time \(t\), the traffic flow can be estimated as \(q = n/t\). Having the average speed of these vehicles \(u\), the traffic density is estimated as \(k = q/u\) with the fundamental relationship \(q = k u\).

As a way of introduction to TFUHP, the field experiment conducted by Sugiyama et al. (2008) will be investigated. Here a fixed number of vehicles \(n\) are driven around a loop road, which is similiar to a roundabout, where no vehicles are allowed to enter or exit the road. In addition, all drivers are instructed to keep a constant speed \(u\) and maintain a fixed distance between themselves and in the vehicle in front of them. This distance is called traffic headway \(h\). A YouTube video of this car-following experiment, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7wm-pZp_mi0, shows the drivers are unable to follow instructions.

Since \(n\) is fixed and speed varies over time and is treated as a random variable \(U\), the fundamental relationship is written as \(Q = nU/l\) where the road length is \(l = 2\pi r\) with \(r\) equal to the radius of the loop road. Returning to the question of the better model, \(\pi = P(Q = q)\) or \(\pi = P(K = k)\), the answer is \(\pi = P(U = u)\). The root causes of traffic breakdown in this experiment are: (1) the inability of the drivers to maintain a constant speed, and (2) the drivers need to accelerate and decelerate to maintain a safe distance between themselves and the vehicles in front of them or safe headways. The optimum driving condition is defined to be time it takes an indivual driver to complete a loop at speed \(u\). The delay is estimated as the extra time for a driver to complete a loop. To explain this scenario, the stationary time-series model of \(U\) is introduced and the calibration of this model is discussed.

In addition to the investigation of the loop road, which is called the Ring Road Experiment, highway bottlenecks and traffic merging are discussed. Here, a combination deterministic and probabilistic models are investigated. Visual imagry including the use of computer simulation and animation are used to increase understanding of complex traffic behavior. This is an open-source project where users are encouraged to freely use this material. https://github.com/PJOssenbruggen/GP. https://github.com/PJOssenbruggen/Basic.

Analytical Tools

The TFUHP are derived and developed from a diverse set of resources:

  • Traffic Analysis: Banks (1998), Daganzo (1997), Elefteriadou (2014), Trieber and Kesting (2013)

  • Probability and Statistics: Agresti (1990), Hosmer and Lemeshow (1989), Iacus (2008), Vandeale (1983)

  • Statistical Computing and Graphics: R Core Team (2014), Iacus (2016), Ossenbruggen (2018), Wickham (2016), RStudio, Inc (2014)
